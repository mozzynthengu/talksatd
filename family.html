Introduction: Words of Thankfulness for Our Families

Brothers and sisters, as we gather today to reflect on the teachings of President Dallin H. Oaks about the family-centered gospel of Jesus Christ, I want to begin by expressing my heartfelt gratitude for the gift of family. Each of us is blessed to have been born into a family—an eternal unit ordained of God.

Our families are not just the places where we first learn to walk, talk, and love; they are the foundational setting where we begin to understand our divine identity and our purpose in God’s plan. Whether our families are perfect or face challenges, they remain our first and most important teachers.

Growing up as a child, I felt deeply how important family is to God’s plan. In the everyday moments—meals together, family prayers, small acts of love and correction—I came to know that family life is not just part of the gospel; it is central to it. Even in times of difficulty, I felt the quiet strength that comes from being part of something eternal, something divinely designed.

Therefore, there is a need—we must cherish our families. We must protect them, nurture them, and recognize their sacred role in our personal growth and eternal destiny. In a world that often distracts or devalues the family unit, we as disciples of Christ are called to remember its eternal purpose and do all we can to preserve and strengthen it.

Recently, just after general conference, a friend asked me if I had listened to President Oaks’ talk. I said, “Yes,” and began to share how grateful I felt for his words—how powerfully they reminded me of the beauty and eternal purpose of family. But before I could say much more, she cut me off and said, “Wait until you get married and see your husband cheating on you.”

Her words hit me hard.

It reminded me that not everyone hears a talk like that with the same heart. For some, the idea of eternal families is painful. Some carry real wounds—loss, betrayal, disappointment, and heartbreak. And yet, even in those moments, I believe the doctrine still holds power. Because as President Oaks gently reminded us, we are a family church. Even when families are broken, incomplete, or hurting, God’s plan still includes us. There is still hope. There is healing in Jesus Christ, and there is always a path forward through Him.

I. The Eternal Nature of Families and Temple Ordinances

President Oaks taught that essential to our doctrine on the family is the temple. In the temple, sacred ordinances and covenants are made that bind us together forever—not just for this life but for eternity.

Our belief in eternal families means that marriage and family relationships can continue beyond the grave through the sealing powers of the priesthood, restored by the Lord.

Even with challenges and trials, the temple stands as a symbol of permanence and divine intention. It reminds us that no matter what has happened, it is never too late to be sealed as eternal families.

II. The Family Proclamation and God’s Plan

The Family Proclamation, announced 30 years ago, declares that the family is ordained of God and central to His plan. It affirms that marriage between a man and a woman is essential and that God’s commandment to multiply and replenish the earth remains in force.

President Oaks reminds us that the purpose of the gospel plan is to exalt the family. This means our mortal life, our choices, and our covenants all work toward the goal of eternal family unity.

Though the world questions or rejects these truths, we stand firmly on God’s revealed plan.

III. Challenges to Families in Today’s World

We recognize that marriage and childbearing are declining in many places, including among Church members. Modern distractions, cultural shifts, and busy lifestyles make it harder to prioritize family.

President Oaks highlighted that where the world moves away from these values, Latter-day Saints should improve and strengthen them.

Families used to work together toward common goals, which unified them. Today, many live more isolated lives under the same roof.

But even amid these challenges, we can build strong families by being intentional, turning off distractions, and choosing faith and unity.

IV. Teaching and Strengthening Families

Parents and guardians are the master teachers. Even when families are broken or incomplete, teaching gospel truths and eternal principles can bless all family members.

President Oaks shared his own experience of losing his father young and being raised by a faithful mother who taught him about eternal families. This example reminds us that imperfect circumstances don’t prevent God’s plan from working.

We can strengthen families by praying together, worshiping together, sharing family stories, teaching life skills, and simply spending quality time.

Sometimes what children want most is not things, but time with us.

V. The Savior as the Ultimate Role Model

Jesus Christ is the perfect example of love, service, patience, and sacrifice—the qualities that build eternal families.

By following Him, we can overcome selfishness and individualism, and find healing for broken hearts.

The Savior’s Atonement empowers us to forgive, change, and grow—even when families struggle.

Our hope lies in Him and the promises of eternal families through temple sealing and the gospel covenant path.

Conclusion

Brothers and sisters, though the world may challenge and weaken family bonds, through the Savior and the restored gospel, we can build strong, eternal families.

Let us cherish, protect, and nurture our families. Let us follow Jesus Christ, who leads us home—together.

In the name of Jesus Christ, amen.
